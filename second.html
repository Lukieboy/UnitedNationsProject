Goal 13: Climate Action

What is Goal 13?

Goal 13, is one of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (UNSDGs), which aims to "Take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts". Climate change is a pressing problem caused by rising temperatures caused by greenhouse gas emissions, and due to the dangerous increase in climate change, taking action against it climate change is crucial for the survival of our planet, and everyone on it.

Why is Goal 13 important?

Goal 13 is extremely important to preserving our entire planet, and the entire human species. Due to humans polluting, deforestating, using fossil fuels, littering, etc, climate change has led to an increase in natural disasters, sea-level rise, ecosystem disruption, food and water insecurity, and severe health impacts, as well as destroying the planet we live on. It calls for urgent actions to reduce emissions, transition into renewable energy, and implement adaptation strategies, to help preserve our planet and build a sustainable and resilient future.



Targets

Target 13.1

Strengthen resilience and adaptive capacity to climate-related hazards and natural disasters in all countries.

Target 13.2

Integrate climate change measures into national policies, strategies and planning.

Target 13.3

Improve education, awareness-raising and human and institutional capacity on climate change mitigation, adaptation, impact reduction and early warning.

Target 13.a

Implement the commitment undertaken by developed-country parties to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change to a goal of mobilizing jointly $100 billion annually by 2020 from all sources to address the needs of developing countries in the context of meaningful mitigation actions and transparency on implementation and fully operationalize the Green Climate Fund through its capitalization as soon as possible.

Target 13.b

Promote mechanisms for raising capacity for effective climate change-related planning and management in least developed countries and small island developing States, including focusing on women, youth and local and marginalized communities.

How to help

Amazon Rainforest Conservancy

Scientists are raising the alarm. The humidity levels of the Amazon are dropping. The wet-adapted tree species and the animals that live there are in crisis. The Amazon rainforest has reached a tipping point and is being reduced to a degraded savannah. Essentially, the Amazon rainforest is dying.
